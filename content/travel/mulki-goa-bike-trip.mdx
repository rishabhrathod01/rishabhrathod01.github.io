I did not set out to write a cautionary tale. I set out to ride a **Harley-Davidson 440X** from **Bangalore to Goa**, stop in **Karwar**, fly a drone I had waited months to hold in India, learn to surf in **Mulki**, and come home with memory cards full of coastline. I came home with most of that — minus one drowned aircraft and a bruised sense of invincibility.

This is the story of that week and a half.

## The bike and the map

Long coastal rides change how you think about distance. You stop measuring the day in meetings and start measuring it in **tanks of fuel**, **curves**, and whether the wind smells like salt yet.

I plotted the rough route and pins on Google Maps as I went; if you want the same breadcrumb trail, here is **[where I actually went](https://maps.app.goo.gl/EgfBWgNmVEohtMyCA)**.

After Goa and Mulki, I rode to **Mysore** to **meet Raksha**, a college friend — we caught up over lunch, I rested for a bit, then pushed back to **Bangalore** the same evening and rolled in around **eight or nine**. The **NICE Road** stretch was miserable on two wheels that night: I spent most of it on the **service road**, tired and ready to be done.

## How the drone even got to Goa

Part of the trip was a **company offsite** in Goa. Months earlier I had **bought the drone in the United States** and had it **delivered to a colleague’s address** there. He carried it over for the offsite. When I finally unboxed it in India, it felt less like a gadget and more like a delayed promise: _okay, now you can actually see this coast from above._

## First flights: Vagator, then Cola and Cabo de Rama

The honest first flight happened on **Vagator**. Sand underfoot, wind indecisive, heart rate too high for something that weighs a few hundred grams. After that I flew at **Cola** and **Cabo de Rama** too — quieter headlands, different light, the same beginner’s grin behind the remote.

## North Goa: hostels, Arambol, and a trek that felt like summer camp

Hostels are where solo trips become shared jokes. I met people in **North Goa**, ended up on a **trek around Arambol**, and carried the drone along like a third wheel that everyone tolerated because the views were worth it. I got **a few shots there** and cut **a trek reel** before schedules diverged and we said goodbye the way travellers always do — _text me if you’re ever in my city_, knowing half the numbers will go cold.

## South Goa: Palolem, a German photographer, and a voice in a beachside cafe

I moved south and stayed **two or three nights** in a **dorm** on **Palolem**. In the bunk across from mine was a **German photographer**; we traded small talk about lenses and tide times the way people trade cigarettes on balconies.

I flew over **Palolem** too — softer light, slower afternoons.

One evening I wandered into a **cafe near the beach** where someone was singing in a **blues-or-jazz register** — I never pinned down the genre, only that I **liked his voice** enough to stay put for **two or three hours** with a drink going warm. I watched the **sunset from Palolem every night** I was there. Some trips are loud; Palolem, for me, turned the volume down.

## Mulki: tent, surf van, and the word “SURFFFINGGGG”

From Palolem I rode to **Mulki** for what I had budgeted as **about seven days of surfing**, starting in a **tent** because I wanted the trip to feel a little rough at the edges.

It did. The tent nights felt **properly adventurous** — canvas flapping, unfamiliar sounds, the sea close enough to taste. I fell in with a group that was **half Bangalore, half Mumbai**; we got along immediately. The next night I **moved into a dorm** for sleep I actually trusted.

Mornings belonged to the **surf camp van**. It rolled up and someone would yell **“SURFFFINGGGG”** — half alarm clock, half pep rally — and the next batch would shuffle out half-asleep and half-stoked. The van **shuttled us to the beach** and **brought us back** after each session until muscle memory replaced anxiety.

I flew the drone on **that beach** as well. By then I was comfortable enough to be dangerous.

## The shot that cost everything

Confidence is a funny thing with a drone. First you are afraid to leave **line of sight**; then you are chasing **one more frame** with the motors humming low over wet sand.

I **risked the drone for a shot too close to the water**. It **touched down into the beach** in a way saltwater does not forgive. It **stopped working** almost politely, as if it had decided the trip was over before I had.

I rode on anyway — **Mysore**, **Raksha**, **home**. The next day in Bangalore I took it in for a check. The diagnosis was short: **dead**. From first flight to obituary was roughly **a week and a half**.

## What I kept

I kept the **route**, the **reels**, the memory of **“SURFFFINGGGG”** echoing across a parking lot, and the humiliation of knowing I broke my own toy for a clip I did not need.

If you are planning something similar: **patience on service roads**, **sleep before NICE at night**, and **rinse the sand off your ambition** before you send a drone over the surf line. The coast will still be there when you land safely.
